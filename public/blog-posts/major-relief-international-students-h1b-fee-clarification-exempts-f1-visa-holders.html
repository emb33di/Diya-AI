Major Relief for International Students: New H-1B Fee Clarification Exempts F-1 Visa Holders

The federal government has issued crucial clarification on the controversial $100,000 H-1B visa fee, providing significant relief to international students and workers already in the United States. The updated guidance, released by U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS) in October 2025, narrows the scope of who must pay the steep fee following President Trump's September 19 proclamation.[1][2]

Who Is Exempt from the $100,000 Fee

The most significant relief comes for individuals already in the United States on valid visas. The fee does not apply to F-1 students transitioning to H-1B status while in the U.S., current H-1B holders seeking extensions or changing employers, or anyone with petitions filed before September 21, 2025. Additionally, workers who receive H-1B approval in the U.S. and later depart to obtain a visa stamp at a consulate are exempt from the fee.[3][2]

This exemption is particularly critical for the thousands of Indian students studying in the United States who plan to transition from F-1 Optional Practical Training (OPT) to H-1B work authorization. As long as these students are physically present in the U.S. when their employer files the H-1B petition and apply for a change of status rather than consular processing, they will not be subject to the $100,000 charge.[2][4][3]

Who Must Pay the Fee

The $100,000 supplemental fee applies to new H-1B petitions filed on or after September 21, 2025, for workers who are outside the United States at the time of filing and do not already hold a valid H-1B visa stamp. The fee also applies to petitions requesting consular notification or port-of-entry processing, and to cases where USCIS denies a change of status or extension request due to the beneficiary being ineligible.[5][3][2]

Employers seeking to hire foreign nationals directly from abroad, rather than converting existing visa holders, face the full financial burden of this new requirement. The fee targets first-time H-1B applicants located outside U.S. borders, creating a significant new barrier for international recruitment.[6][3][5]

Critical Implications for International Students

For Indian students and families investing in U.S. education with the goal of eventual employment, this guidance provides crucial clarity on post-graduation pathways. Students who secure employment during their F-1 OPT period and remain in the U.S. throughout the H-1B petition and adjudication process will avoid the $100,000 fee entirely.[4][3][2]

However, students must be strategic about travel plans during this transition period. If a student departs the U.S. before their H-1B petition is filed or adjudicated, they risk falling into the category requiring the supplemental fee. The guidance effectively creates a strong incentive for F-1 students to remain in the United States continuously from the time they begin employment search through H-1B approval.[6][3][2]

Payment Process and Exceptions

Employers required to pay the fee must do so through the federal government's pay.gov portal, with proof of payment or approved exception required to accompany every H-1B petition. Petitions submitted without proper documentation of payment or exemption will be denied outright.[3]

The Department of Homeland Security may grant exceptions to the fee only in "extraordinarily rare" circumstances where the worker's presence serves the national interest, no qualified American worker is available, and the fee would significantly undermine U.S. interests. Employers seeking exceptions must submit detailed justifications to H1BExceptions@hq.dhs.gov, though USCIS has emphasized such exceptions will be granted sparingly.[2][3]

Legal Challenges and Future Uncertainty

Multiple lawsuits are pending in federal court challenging the proclamation's legality, including a prominent case filed by the U.S. Chamber of Commerce. Challengers argue the administration lacks authority to impose such a substantial fee without congressional approval. While litigation could potentially overturn or modify the fee structure, it remains in full effect for now, and employers must comply with all requirements when filing new H-1B petitions.[5][3]

The proclamation is set to remain in effect for 12 months from September 21, 2025, with the possibility of extension. Universities, employers, and immigration advocacy organizations continue to monitor both the legal challenges and any additional guidance that may emerge from federal agencies.[7][8][6][2]

Bottom Line for Prospective Students

For Indian students planning to study in the United States with intentions of working there post-graduation, the clarified guidance offers a viable pathway that avoids the $100,000 fee. The key is securing employment and maintaining continuous presence in the U.S. during the F-1 to H-1B transition. Students should work closely with their university international student offices and prospective employers to ensure their petition strategy maximizes their exemption eligibility and minimizes travel risks during the critical transition period.[4][3][2]
